Monosyllabic stems. Before the endings beginning with vowels, final <tt>\u012b</tt> is changed to <tt>iy</tt> and <tt>\u016b</tt> to <tt>uv</tt>; while final <tt>\u0101</tt> is dropped altogether, except in the strong cases, and in the acc. pl., which is like the nominative (according to the grammarians, <tt>\u0101</tt> is lost here also: no instances of the occurrence of such a form appear to be quotable). Stems in <tt>\u012b</tt> and <tt>\u016b</tt> are in the later language allowed to take optionally the fuller endings <tt>\u0101i</tt>, <tt>\u0101s</tt>, <tt>\u0101m</tt> in the singular (dat., abl.-gen., loc.); but no such forms are ever met with in the Veda (except <tt>bhiy\u0101\u00ed</tt> [?], RV., once). Before <tt>\u0101m</tt> of gen. pl., <tt>n</tt> may or may not be inserted; in the Veda it is regularly inserted, with a single exception (<tt>dhiy\u0101\u0301m</tt>, once). The vocative is like the nominative in the singular as well as the other numbers; but instances of its occurrence in uncompounded stems are not found in the Veda, and must be extremely rare everywhere. The earlier Vedic dual ending is <tt>\u0101</tt> instead of <tt>\u0101u</tt>.</p>\n\n350. As to the admissibility of the fuller endings <tt>\u0101i</tt>, <tt>\u0101s</tt>, and <tt>\u0101m</tt> in the singular (feminine), grammatical authorities are somewhat at variance; but they are never found in the Veda, and have been omitted from the above scheme as probably unreal.\n\nb. If two consonants precede the final <tt>\u012b</tt> or <tt>\u016b</tt>, the dissyllabic forms, with <tt>iy</tt> and <tt>uv</tt>, are regularly written; after one consonant, the usage is varying. The grammarians prescribe <tt>iy</tt> and <tt>uv</tt> when the monosyllabic stem has more the character of a noun, and <tt>y</tt> and <tt>v</tt> when it is more purely a verbal root with participial value.
